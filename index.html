2. Problem / Context

Planning postgraduate study abroad is a long journey with too many disconnected steps

For many XJTLU students, study-abroad preparation is not a single task. It is a sequence of research, comparison, document preparation, and deadline management activities that can feel difficult to structure.

The project focuses on a student experience shaped by information overload, unclear priorities, long planning horizons, and emotional uncertainty.

Our design opportunity was to transform this complex process into a guided pathway that still respects academic seriousness while making progress more visible and manageable.

Scattered information sources

Students switch between university websites, social media, notes, and peer advice with no unified planning structure.

Invisible progress

Long-term goals such as applications or language tests provide little short-term feedback, which reduces momentum.

High-stakes comparison work

Comparing destinations, courses, deadlines, and requirements creates cognitive load and decision fatigue.

Uneven support and confidence

Students may receive advice from many channels, but not always at the right time or in the right format for action.

3. Literature Review

Research themes shaping the design direction

Instead of summarising papers one by one, the review is organised by the major themes that informed the product concept and interaction strategy.

Theme 01

Motivation and sustained engagement

Educational motivation research suggests that visible goals, achievable steps, and timely feedback can help users persist with demanding tasks.

Design implication: break the application journey into quests, milestones, and feedback moments rather than one large task list.

Theme 02

Cognitive load and information structure

HCI literature consistently shows that users perform better when complex information is chunked, prioritised, and sequenced clearly.

Design implication: organise study-abroad planning into clear stages with focused actions, not dense information dumps.

Theme 03

Trust, guidance, and decision support

Students need more than motivation alone. They also need reliable guidance, credible information, and checkpoints for reflection.

Design implication: combine playful progression with trustworthy resources, advisor touchpoints, and explicit requirements.

4. Product / Competitor Analysis

Existing tools support parts of the journey, but rarely the whole experience

The review focused on what current products do well, what they overlook, and where a student-centred opportunity remains.

Category A

University and programme websites

  • Strong on official requirements and factual accuracy.
  • Weak on cross-option comparison and long-term motivation.
  • Often structured around institutions, not student journeys.

Category B

Productivity tools and note systems

  • Useful for task tracking and deadline capture.
  • Require users to build their own structure from scratch.
  • Lack domain-specific guidance for postgraduate planning.

Category C

Forums, consultants, and social advice channels

  • Helpful for lived experience and reassurance.
  • Information quality and relevance can vary widely.
  • Advice is often fragmented and difficult to revisit later.

Opportunity Gap

No single product combines structured planning, credible guidance, and motivating progress cues in one student-facing experience

This gap shaped the Journey RPG concept: a system that treats the process as a navigable journey rather than a passive collection of information.

5. Key Insights

The research converged on four design insights

These insights became the bridge between evidence and solution direction.

01

Students need visible progress

Long, uncertain processes become easier to sustain when advancement is shown through clear stages and small wins.

02

Guidance must be actionable

Generic advice is less useful than task-ready prompts connected to the student’s current stage of planning.

03

Motivation should support, not distract

Gamified elements work best when they reinforce meaningful progress instead of competing with serious decision making.

04

Different roles need different visibility

Students want autonomy and reassurance, while advisors need concise evidence of readiness, blockers, and next actions.

6. User Requirements

Requirements translated directly from the research evidence

Each requirement is written as a system obligation so the design remains specific, testable, and grounded in user needs.

Core requirement

System must structure the study-abroad journey into clear stages.

Users need to know what happens first, what comes next, and what counts as progress.

Core requirement

System must make deadlines, tasks, and dependencies visible.

Important milestones should be understandable at a glance rather than hidden in scattered notes.

Trust requirement

System must connect playful interaction with credible academic guidance.

Motivation cues should never reduce the seriousness of application decisions.

Support requirement

System must provide just-in-time prompts for comparison and preparation.

The interface should surface relevant tasks and advice at the point of need.

Communication requirement

System must help advisors understand student readiness quickly.

Status, blockers, and next steps should be legible without reading long descriptions.

Motivation requirement

System must sustain momentum across a long planning period.

Students should feel that meaningful work is happening even before applications are submitted.

7. Persona

Primary and secondary users in the planning ecosystem

The portfolio emphasises one main student persona while still recognising the advisor perspective that influences feedback, confidence, and decision quality.

Primary Persona

Student user

Lin, 20, XJTLU student exploring overseas postgraduate options

Motivated to study abroad, but unsure how to organise research, compare options, and maintain momentum across a long decision timeline.

Goals

  • Understand suitable countries, universities, and programmes.
  • Track progress without relying on fragmented spreadsheets.
  • Feel more confident about what to do next.

Frustrations

  • Too much scattered information and inconsistent advice.
  • Difficulty prioritising tasks over time.
  • Low motivation when effort feels invisible.

Secondary Persona

Advisor user

Academic advisor or mentor supporting student planning

Wants a concise overview of student readiness, current blockers, and the tasks that need intervention or reassurance.

Needs from the system

  • Quick visibility of progress and missing actions.
  • Evidence that a student has moved beyond passive browsing.
  • A simple basis for more focused conversations.

8. User Journey Map

The journey was mapped as stages, pain points, and intervention opportunities

The visual journey map asset can be added later, but the structure below already captures the intended flow and where the product should help most.

Stage 1

Discover

Goal: Understand why, where, and when to apply.

Pain: The scope feels wide and difficult to narrow.

Opportunity: Guided onboarding and starter pathways.

Stage 2

Compare

Goal: Review courses, requirements, and destinations.

Pain: Comparison work becomes cognitively heavy.

Opportunity: Side-by-side criteria and saved options.

Stage 3

Plan

Goal: Turn decisions into milestones and tasks.

Pain: Deadlines and dependencies are easily missed.

Opportunity: Timeline view and readiness checkpoints.

Stage 4

Prepare

Goal: Gather documents, tests, and statements.

Pain: Motivation drops during repetitive preparation work.

Opportunity: Quest framing and milestone feedback.

Stage 5

Apply

Goal: Submit with confidence and track completion.

Pain: Last-stage uncertainty still feels stressful.

Opportunity: Final review prompts and status reassurance.

Early ideation used fast sketching around focused design questions

Crazy 8 encouraged breadth before polish. The goal was to test multiple ways of making planning feel clearer, more motivating, and easier to navigate.

Design questions

How might progress feel visible? How might planning feel less overwhelming? How might guidance stay credible and motivating?

10. Design Alternatives

Three directions were compared before the final concept was selected

The alternatives explore different balances between clarity, motivation, and the seriousness expected of an academic planning tool.

A

Linear checklist dashboard

A structured task manager focused on deadlines, documents, and completion states.

  • Very clear and practical.
  • Low emotional engagement.
  • Feels similar to existing productivity tools.
B

Journey RPG map

A guided pathway that treats preparation as a series of meaningful stages, quests, and readiness milestones.

  • Balances motivation with structure.
  • Makes progress and direction visible.
  • Supports both exploration and action.
C

Social planning hub

A community-led approach centred on peer stories, shared tips, and collaborative support.

  • Useful for reassurance and experience sharing.
  • Harder to maintain quality and consistency.
  • Less suitable for clear task sequencing.

Why B was chosen

Option B offered the strongest balance of motivation, structure, and academic credibility

The Journey RPG direction retained the seriousness of application planning while introducing a clearer sense of progression than Option A and a more dependable structure than Option C.

11. Design Solution / Product

The final direction presents study-abroad planning as a guided journey

Journey RPG reframes the product as a progression system where each stage helps students understand goals, complete tasks, and build confidence.

The selected concept combines a map-like progression model with practical planning support. Rather than replacing academic seriousness, the design uses the language of journeys and quests to make effort feel more coherent.

Quest-based roadmap

Major preparation tasks are grouped into stages so students can see how each step contributes to the larger goal.

Comparison toolkit

Students can review programmes, destinations, and requirements without losing track of their shortlist.

Progress and readiness states

Status cues help users understand what is complete, what is blocked, and what needs attention next.

Advisor touchpoints

Key checkpoints make it easier to convert broad support into focused conversations and timely feedback.

14. Evaluation

Evaluation focuses on what the concept communicates well and what still needs refinement

This section is structured to make findings and improvements visible at a glance, ready to absorb stronger evidence such as user quotes or testing screenshots later.

Method

Peer walkthroughs

Used to test whether the journey structure and page hierarchy feel understandable.

Method

Heuristic review

Used to assess clarity, consistency, and whether gamified elements remain appropriate.

Method

Content critique

Used to identify where task guidance and comparison information still need sharpening.

Key findings

01

The journey metaphor improves engagement, but users still need direct access to deadlines and next actions.

02

Comparison support is essential because motivation alone does not reduce decision complexity.

03

Playful elements are most credible when paired with explicit requirements and advisor checkpoints.

Improvements identified

Strengthen progress visibility

Add clearer stage completion states and a stronger sense of readiness.

Clarify comparison workflows

Support shortlisting, criteria weighting, and requirement review more directly.

Refine reward intensity

Keep feedback motivating, but avoid visual elements that feel too game-like for academic planning.

15. Reflection

The process highlighted both the promise and the limits of gamified planning

Reflection focuses on what the team learned, what still needs evidence, and how ethical considerations shaped the design discussion.

Learning

Designing for motivation requires structural clarity first

The most valuable design move was not adding playful visuals, but re-organising the journey into a form that users can understand and act on.

Limitations

The concept still needs richer real-world validation

Future iterations should include deeper user testing, higher-fidelity task flows, and stronger evidence around how students compare options over time.

Ethics and AI use

Playful systems must remain transparent, supportive, and accountable

Any AI-assisted content, planning suggestions, or design support should be checked against user evidence, course expectations, and the risk of oversimplifying high-stakes decisions.
